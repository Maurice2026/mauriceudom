10 Must-Read Nigerian Plays Every Literature Student Should Know

A curated reading list of the plays that defined Nigerian drama — from Nobel laureates to the bold new voices shaping the future of African theatre.

Nigerian drama is one of the richest theatrical traditions in the world. From Wole Soyinka's mythological landscapes to the sharp social satire of younger playwrights, Nigerian plays have confronted power, corruption, gender, tradition, and the complexities of post-colonial identity with a force and originality that demands global attention.

Whether you are a literature student preparing for examinations, a theatre lover exploring African drama, or a writer studying the craft of playwriting, these ten plays are essential reading. Each one represents a different dimension of the Nigerian dramatic tradition — and each will change how you think about theatre.

01
Death and the King's Horseman
Wole Soyinka (1975)

Based on a true historical event in colonial Oyo, this play explores the clash between Yoruba metaphysical belief and British colonial authority when the king's horseman is prevented from completing a ritual suicide. Soyinka insists the play is not about cultural conflict but about the failure of will — a distinction that makes it endlessly rich for study and debate.

This is arguably the finest play ever written by an African. It earned Soyinka the Nobel Prize for Literature in 1986 and remains a cornerstone of world dramatic literature.

02
The Lion and the Jewel
Wole Soyinka (1963)

A charming comedy set in the village of Ilujinle, where a schoolteacher and a traditional chief compete for the affections of the village beauty, Sidi. Beneath its humour lies a sharp critique of the tension between modernity and tradition — a theme that runs through all of Nigerian literature. This play is accessible, theatrical, and one of the best introductions to Nigerian drama.

ComedyTradition vs Modernity
03
The Gods Are Not to Blame
Ola Rotimi (1968)

Rotimi's masterful adaptation of Sophocles' Oedipus Rex transplants the Greek tragedy into a Yoruba setting, proving that the themes of fate, pride, and self-destruction are universal. King Odewale's journey from heroic warrior to tragic figure is one of the most powerful arcs in African drama. The play is widely studied in universities across Nigeria and West Africa.

TragedyFateAdaptation
04
The Marriage of Anansewa
Efua Sutherland (1975)

While Sutherland is Ghanaian, this play is widely studied across West African literature programmes and is essential reading for anyone studying the dramatic traditions of the region. Using the Ananse storytelling tradition, Sutherland creates a witty, participatory play about a father who auctions his daughter's hand in marriage to the highest-bidding chief. It is a masterclass in using traditional performance forms within modern theatre.

ComedyWest AfricanStorytelling
05
Our Husband Has Gone Mad Again
Ola Rotimi (1977)

A rollicking comedy about a retired military officer turned politician whose three wives — each from a different social class — create chaos when they discover each other. Rotimi uses the domestic farce to satirise political ambition, class pretension, and gender dynamics in post-independence Nigeria. The dialogue is electric and the staging demands are brilliantly inventive.

ComedyPolitical Satire
06
Wedlock of the Gods
Zulu Sofola (1972)

One of the earliest published plays by a Nigerian woman, Wedlock of the Gods tells the story of a young widow forced by tradition to marry her late husband's brother — while secretly in love with another man. Sofola's unflinching examination of patriarchal customs and the suffering of women under rigid tradition opened a door that many Nigerian women playwrights have since walked through.

TragedyGenderTradition
07
The Blind Gods
Maurice Udom

A searing tragedy that exposes the deep-rooted injustice against women in patriarchal societies. The play examines how tradition, power, and silence conspire to destroy the lives of women who dare to demand justice — and how the gods themselves seem blind to their suffering. It speaks beyond any single community to every society where corruption has become the architecture of daily life.

TragedyGender JusticePower
08
Morountodun
Femi Osofisan (1982)

Osofisan reimagines the Yoruba heroine Moremi in the context of modern class struggle and worker exploitation. A young university student infiltrates a peasant revolt to betray it — but finds her loyalties challenged as she witnesses the genuine suffering of the oppressed. The play is a powerful critique of class, privilege, and the moral cost of complicity.

Political DramaClassRevisionist
09
The Golden Home
Maurice Udom

A biting comedy of greed and self-delusion. Aladima, a university graduate reduced to working as a gatekeeper, blackmails his employers over their secret affairs — then concocts a scheme to steal their oil spill compensation millions. The play holds a hilarious and devastating mirror to corruption, ambition, and the spectacular consequences of overreaching.

SatireComedyCorruption
10
Emotan
Ola Rotimi (2003)

Rotimi's final major work dramatises the legend of Emotan, the Benin market woman whose bravery helped restore a rightful king to the throne. The play celebrates female heroism, political courage, and the power of ordinary people to reshape history. It is a fitting capstone to the career of one of Nigeria's greatest playwrights.

How to approach these plays: Do not read them as historical documents — read them as living works of art. Pay attention to the dramatic structure, the language, the use of music and ritual, and the relationship between the play and its cultural context. Ask yourself: what is the playwright arguing? Who has power and who does not? What traditions are being questioned? Nigerian drama at its best does not merely entertain — it interrogates the world and demands that the audience respond.
