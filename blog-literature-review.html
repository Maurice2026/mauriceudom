How to Structure a Literature Review: From Reading to Writing

A practical walkthrough for postgraduate students — from searching for sources to writing a review that demonstrates critical thinking.

The literature review is where most dissertation students get stuck. Not because they cannot find sources — but because they do not know how to organise dozens of articles, books, and papers into a coherent narrative that demonstrates their understanding of the field.

A literature review is not a summary of everything you have read. It is an argument about the current state of knowledge on your topic — what is known, what is debated, what is missing, and where your research fits in. This guide walks you through the entire process in five clear steps.

01
Define Your Scope

Before you read a single article, define what your literature review will cover and what it will not. Your scope should be narrow enough to be manageable but broad enough to provide context for your research question.

Ask yourself: What is my research question? What key concepts or variables am I exploring? What time period is relevant? What geographical or disciplinary boundaries should I set? Write these boundaries down. They will prevent you from drowning in an ocean of tangentially related sources.

02
Search Systematically

Use academic databases — Google Scholar, JSTOR, PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, or your university library portal — to find relevant sources. Start with your key terms, then follow the reference lists of the most relevant articles to find older foundational studies. Also check who has cited those key articles to find newer work.

As you read, keep a structured record of each source: author, year, title, key findings, methodology, and your own notes on how it relates to your research question. A spreadsheet or citation manager (Zotero, Mendeley) makes this much easier than handwritten notes.

03
Organise by Themes, Not by Source

This is the most important step and where most students go wrong. A literature review should be organised thematically — by the ideas, debates, and findings that emerge across multiple sources — not source by source.

Source-by-source (wrong): "Smith (2018) found X. Jones (2019) found Y. Ahmed (2020) found Z." This reads like an annotated bibliography, not a review. It shows you have read the sources but not that you can synthesise them.
Thematic (correct)
Several studies have identified a negative correlation between social media usage and academic performance (Smith, 2018; Ahmed, 2020; Osei, 2022). However, this relationship appears to be moderated by the type of platform used, with educational platforms showing neutral or positive effects (Jones, 2019; Li & Chen, 2021).

Notice how the thematic approach groups sources around ideas rather than listing them individually. This demonstrates critical thinking — the ability to see patterns, contradictions, and gaps across the literature.

04
Write with Critical Analysis

A literature review is not a neutral report. You are expected to evaluate the sources — to comment on their strengths, weaknesses, and limitations. This does not mean attacking every study. It means showing that you can assess the quality and relevance of evidence.

Example of Critical Analysis
While Smith's (2018) study provides valuable quantitative data on the correlation between screen time and grades, its cross-sectional design limits causal inference. Ahmed (2020) addressed this limitation through a longitudinal approach, tracking 500 students over two academic years, and confirmed the negative association — but only among students in their first year of study.

Notice the language: "provides valuable data," "limits causal inference," "addressed this limitation." You are engaging with the research, not just reporting it.

05
Identify the Gap

The literature review should build toward a clear conclusion: here is what we know, here is what we do not know, and here is where my research fits in. The "gap" is the specific question or area that existing research has not adequately addressed — and it is the justification for your entire study.

Identifying the Gap
While existing research has established a correlation between social media usage and academic performance, most studies have been conducted in Western university contexts. There remains a significant gap in understanding how this relationship operates in developing nations, where internet access patterns, device ownership, and educational infrastructure differ substantially. This study addresses that gap by examining the relationship among university students in three African countries.

Common Mistakes

1. Summarising instead of synthesising. Listing what each author said is not a literature review. Group sources by theme and show how they relate to each other.
2. Including irrelevant sources. Every source in your review should directly relate to your research question. Padding with loosely related studies weakens your argument.
3. Failing to show the gap. If your review does not lead to a clear justification for your research, it has not done its job.
4. Relying only on recent sources. Include foundational studies that established key concepts, even if they are decades old. Balance seminal works with current research.
5. Not being critical enough. Presenting all studies as equally valid suggests you lack the ability to evaluate evidence. Note methodological strengths and limitations.
A practical structure for your literature review:

Opening paragraph: Define the topic and its importance. State the scope of the review.
Theme 1: First major area of research — what studies have found, areas of agreement.
Theme 2: Second major area — contrasting findings, debates, unresolved questions.
Theme 3: Third area — methodological approaches, their strengths and limitations.
Closing paragraph: Summarise the state of knowledge. Identify the gap. State how your research addresses it.
