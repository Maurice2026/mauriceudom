Modal Verbs in English: A Complete Guide to Mastering All Five Functions

Ability, permission, advice, obligation, and possibility — understand when and how to use every modal verb correctly.

Sunday, April 27, 2026 · 10 min read

Modal verbs are some of the most frequently used words in the English language, yet they remain a constant source of confusion for learners and even native speakers. Words like can, could, may, might, should, must, and have to appear in nearly every conversation you will ever have in English. They shape meaning in subtle but powerful ways — the difference between "Aniekan can leave" and "Aniekan must leave" changes the entire tone of a sentence. Understanding modal verbs is not just a grammar exercise. It is a communication skill that affects how clearly and confidently you express yourself in writing, at work, in exams, and in everyday speech.

This guide breaks down the five core functions of modal verbs — ability, permission, advice, obligation, and possibility — with clear explanations, real-world examples, and common mistakes to avoid.

Quick Reference: Modal Verbs at a Glance

Before diving into the details, here is a summary table showing each function, the modal verbs associated with it, and a sample sentence.

Now let us explore each function in depth.

1. Ability — Can and Could

When you want to talk about what someone is able to do, you reach for can or could. The distinction between the two is largely about time. "Can" describes a present or general ability — something you are capable of doing right now or as a permanent skill. "Could" shifts that ability into the past, describing something you were once able to do.

Examples

Afiong can play the piano beautifully. (present ability)

When Okon was younger, he could run a mile in under six minutes. (past ability)

Aniedi can code in Python, Java, and C++. (general skill)

Mfoniso could already read by the age of four. (past achievement)

One important nuance: "could" can also express a polite or tentative version of ability. Saying "I could help you with that, Ekaette" is softer than "I can help you with that." This makes "could" a versatile word that bridges ability and politeness — which leads naturally into our next function.

2. Permission — May, Can, and Could

Asking for or granting permission is one of the most common social functions of language, and modal verbs handle it with varying levels of formality. "Can" is the most casual. "Could" adds politeness. "May" is the most formal and traditional choice.

Examples

Idongesit, can I borrow your charger? (casual, among friends)

Could I leave early today, Obong Ndem? (polite, workplace setting)

May I see the latest draft, Iniobong? (formal, professional or academic)

Eka, can Uduak and I go to the library? (everyday family request)

In everyday spoken English, most people use "can" for permission without thinking twice. However, in formal writing, academic settings, or professional emails, choosing "may" signals respect and awareness of register. If you are writing a cover letter, an academic paper, or a formal request, "may" is almost always the better choice.

💡 Can vs. May — Does It Really Matter?

You may have heard the old classroom correction: "You can go, but may you?" Strictly speaking, "can" refers to ability and "may" to permission. In modern English, though, "can" is widely accepted for both. The distinction matters most in formal contexts — job interviews, official correspondence, and academic writing — where precision earns respect.

3. Advice — Should

"Should" is the modal verb of recommendations. When you use it, you are telling someone what you believe is the best course of action — without forcing them. It carries the weight of a strong suggestion without crossing into obligation. This makes "should" one of the most useful modal verbs in professional communication, where giving direct orders can come across as rude.

Examples

Uduak should try a different font for the heading.

Mbakara should review his notes before the exam.

Enobong and Aniefiok should schedule a follow-up meeting next week.

Aritaundu, you should apply for that scholarship.

"Should" also works in the negative to advise against something. "Nsikak shouldn't submit the report without proofreading it" is a polite but clear warning. In academic and professional writing, "should" appears frequently in recommendations, conclusions, and policy suggestions — making it essential for anyone who writes reports, proposals, or research papers.

4. Obligation — Must and Have To

When something is not optional, you need "must" or "have to." Both express obligation, but the flavour differs. "Must" tends to convey an internal or authoritative obligation — a rule imposed by the speaker or an institution. "Have to" often implies an external requirement — something forced by circumstances or someone else's rules.

Examples

Aniekan and Emem must follow the style guide. (company rule, authoritative tone)

Imo has to finish the assignment by Friday. (external deadline)

Ekaette must submit her thesis before the end of the month. (institutional deadline)

Utibe and Afiong have to attend the orientation session. (mandatory requirement)

✗ Common Mistake

Okon mustn't pay for the tickets. (intending: it's not necessary)

✓ Correct

Okon doesn't have to pay for the tickets. (meaning: it's not necessary)

This is one of the trickiest areas of modal verb usage. "Mustn't" does not mean "not necessary" — it means "prohibited." Saying "Mfoniso mustn't touch that" means it is forbidden. Saying "Mfoniso doesn't have to touch that" means there is no requirement to do so. Mixing these up can cause serious misunderstandings, especially in professional or legal contexts.

5. Possibility — Might, May, and Could

The final function covers situations where something is possible but not certain. English has three modal verbs for this, and they occupy a spectrum of likelihood. "May" suggests a reasonable chance — perhaps a 40 to 60 percent probability. "Might" is slightly less certain, expressing a weaker possibility. "Could" indicates that something is within the range of possibility, though often less likely.

Examples

Nsikak's project might take longer than expected. (uncertain, but possible)

Iniobong may request revisions on the design. (reasonable possibility)

Afiong's approach could solve the problem. (one possible outcome)

Enobong might travel to Lagos next month. (not yet decided)

In academic writing, these verbs are critical for hedging — the practice of avoiding absolute claims. Instead of writing "this treatment cures the disease," a researcher like Aniedi might write "this treatment may reduce symptoms." That single word protects the writer from overclaiming and signals intellectual honesty. If you write essays, research papers, or reports of any kind, mastering the possibility modals will immediately improve the quality of your prose.

Why Modal Verbs Matter Beyond Grammar Class

Modal verbs are not just a topic for textbooks and exams. They shape how people perceive your communication in real life. Using "must" when you mean "should" makes you sound demanding. Using "can" when "may" is appropriate can make your writing feel too casual. Choosing "might" over "will" shows nuance and careful thinking.

In the workplace, modal verbs determine tone in emails, reports, and meetings. A team leader like Obong Ndem who writes "Aniefiok, you should consider this approach" encourages collaboration. One who writes "Aniefiok, you must do this" issues a command. Both have their place, but knowing which one to use — and when — is what separates strong communicators from everyone else.

For students preparing for exams like IELTS, TOEFL, or Cambridge English, modal verbs appear in reading, writing, listening, and speaking sections. Examiners pay close attention to how accurately and naturally you use them. A strong command of modals can push your score from good to excellent.

Quick Tips for Using Modal Verbs Correctly

First, modal verbs are always followed by the base form of the verb — never add "to" after a modal. You say "Ekaette can swim," not "Ekaette can to swim." The only exception is "have to," which requires "to" because "have" functions as a regular verb in this construction.

Second, modal verbs do not change form for different subjects. There is no "he cans" or "she musts." The modal stays the same regardless of whether the subject is I, you, he, she, we, or they.

Third, when forming negatives, simply add "not" after the modal: cannot, should not, must not, might not. In contractions, these become can't, shouldn't, mustn't, and mightn't — though mightn't is rare in modern English.

Finally, practice recognising modal verbs in context. Read articles, listen to podcasts, and pay attention to how speakers choose between "can" and "could," or between "must" and "should." The more exposure you get, the more intuitive your choices will become.

Modal verbs may be small words, but they carry enormous weight in communication. They are the difference between a suggestion and a command, between certainty and speculation, between formality and friendliness. Mastering them gives you control over not just what you say, but how it lands. Whether you are writing an essay, composing a professional email, or preparing for an English exam, these five functions — ability, permission, advice, obligation, and possibility — are tools you will reach for every single day.
