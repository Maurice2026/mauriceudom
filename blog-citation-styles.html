APA vs MLA vs Chicago: Which Citation Style Should You Use?

A quick, clear guide to the three most common citation styles — so you never format a reference incorrectly again.

Citation styles exist for one reason: to give credit to the sources you use in a consistent, verifiable way. But with multiple styles in use across different disciplines, choosing the right one — and formatting it correctly — can be confusing.

This guide compares the three most widely used citation styles: APA (American Psychological Association), MLA (Modern Language Association), and Chicago/Turabian. You will learn when to use each, how they differ, and see formatting examples you can follow immediately.

APA Style (7th Edition)

Best for: Psychology, education, social sciences, nursing, business, and any discipline where recency of research matters. APA emphasises the date of publication because in scientific fields, newer research often supersedes older work.

In-text citation format: (Author's Last Name, Year). For direct quotes, add the page number: (Author, Year, p. 45).

In-Text Example
Research has shown a strong correlation between sleep quality and academic performance (Walker, 2017).
Reference List Entry — Book
Walker, M. (2017). Why we sleep: Unlocking the power of sleep and dreams. Scribner.
Reference List Entry — Journal Article
Okafor, A., & Chen, L. (2024). Digital platform usage and student performance: A cross-cultural study. Journal of Educational Technology, 48(3), 112–128. https://doi.org/10.xxxx

Key rules: Use hanging indent for references. Italicise book titles and journal names. Include DOI for journal articles when available. Use "&" between authors in parenthetical citations but "and" in narrative citations.

MLA Style (9th Edition)

Best for: Literature, languages, cultural studies, philosophy, religion, and the arts. MLA focuses on authorship and the text itself rather than when it was published, because in the humanities, a work from 1850 can be as relevant as one from 2024.

In-text citation format: (Author's Last Name Page Number) — no comma, no "p."

In-Text Example
The novel explores the tension between tradition and individual desire (Achebe 45).
Works Cited Entry — Book
Achebe, Chinua. Things Fall Apart. Heinemann, 1958.
Works Cited Entry — Journal Article
Okafor, Amaka, and Li Chen. "Digital Platform Usage and Student Performance." Journal of Educational Technology, vol. 48, no. 3, 2024, pp. 112–28.

Key rules: Use hanging indent. Italicise book and journal titles. Use quotation marks for article titles. Include the publisher for books. List the author's full name (Last, First).

Chicago Style (17th Edition)

Best for: History, some humanities, and publishing. Chicago offers two systems: Notes-Bibliography (footnotes with a bibliography, preferred in humanities) and Author-Date (similar to APA, used in sciences). Most students encounter the Notes-Bibliography system.

Notes-Bibliography — Footnote format: A superscript number in the text links to a footnote at the bottom of the page with full source details.

In-Text
The economic impact was devastating for rural communities.¹
Footnote
1. Amina Osei, Colonial Trade and Its Aftermath (Accra: University Press, 2019), 134.
Bibliography Entry
Osei, Amina. Colonial Trade and Its Aftermath. Accra: University Press, 2019.

Key rules: Footnotes use full details on first citation, shortened form after. Bibliography is alphabetical by author's last name. Footnote and bibliography formats differ slightly (note the comma vs period differences).

How to Choose

In most cases, you do not choose — your institution, department, or journal tells you which style to use. Always check the assignment guidelines or journal submission requirements before you begin writing. If no style is specified, follow this general rule: social sciences use APA, humanities use MLA or Chicago, and history uses Chicago Notes-Bibliography.

The most important thing is consistency. Whichever style you use, apply it uniformly throughout your entire paper. Mixing styles is worse than using the wrong one.

Practical tools: Citation managers like Zotero (free), Mendeley (free), and EndNote save enormous time by automatically formatting your references. Google Scholar also provides pre-formatted citations — click the quotation mark icon below any search result. However, always double-check auto-generated citations against the official style guide, as errors are common.
