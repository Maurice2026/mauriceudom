APA vs MLA vs Chicago: Which Citation Style Should You Use?

A clear comparison of the three major citation styles — with examples and a quick guide to choosing the right one for your discipline.

Citation styles exist for one reason: to give credit to the sources you use, consistently and transparently. But for students encountering them for the first time, the differences between APA, MLA, and Chicago can feel arbitrary and confusing. They are not. Each style was designed for a specific academic community, and once you understand the logic behind them, choosing and applying the right one becomes straightforward.

APA Style

APA is the dominant citation style in the social sciences. It emphasises the date of publication because in fields like psychology, education, and nursing, the recency of research matters — a study from 2024 may supersede one from 2014.

In-Text Citation
Research has shown that sleep deprivation significantly impairs academic performance (Walker, 2017).

Walker (2017) found that students who slept fewer than six hours performed 23% worse on examinations.
Reference List Entry (Book)
Walker, M. (2017). Why we sleep: The new science of sleep and dreams. Penguin Books.
Reference List Entry (Journal Article)
Smith, J., & Lee, K. (2023). Digital literacy and student engagement in higher education. Journal of Educational Technology, 45(2), 112–128. https://doi.org/10.1000/example

Key features: Author's surname and year in-text. Full reference list at the end. DOIs included for online sources. Title of article not italicised; title of journal italicised.

MLA Style

MLA is the standard in the humanities — literature, languages, philosophy, and cultural studies. It emphasises page numbers rather than dates because in these fields, the specific passage matters more than when it was written. A Shakespeare scholar needs to cite Act 3, Scene 1 — not the year of publication.

In-Text Citation
The novel explores "the impossibility of return" as a central theme of the diaspora experience (Adichie 45).

Adichie argues that identity is shaped not by geography alone but by the stories we tell about where we come from (112).
Works Cited Entry (Book)
Adichie, Chimamanda Ngozi. Americanah. Alfred A. Knopf, 2013.
Works Cited Entry (Journal Article)
Smith, John, and Karen Lee. "Digital Literacy and Student Engagement in Higher Education." Journal of Educational Technology, vol. 45, no. 2, 2023, pp. 112–128.

Key features: Author and page number in-text (no comma, no "p."). Article titles in quotation marks. "Works Cited" (not "References" or "Bibliography"). Publisher and year at end.

Chicago Style

Chicago is the most flexible of the three, offering two distinct systems. The Notes-Bibliography system uses footnotes and is preferred in history, arts, and publishing. The Author-Date system is similar to APA and is used in some social sciences. Most students encounter the footnote system.

Footnote (Notes-Bibliography)
¹ Matthew Walker, Why We Sleep: The New Science of Sleep and Dreams (London: Penguin Books, 2017), 134.
Bibliography Entry
Walker, Matthew. Why We Sleep: The New Science of Sleep and Dreams. London: Penguin Books, 2017.

Key features: Footnotes at the bottom of the page (numbered). Full bibliographic details in the first footnote; shortened form in subsequent references. Bibliography at the end lists all sources alphabetically.

Which Should You Use?

The simple rule: Use whichever style your professor, department, or target journal requires. If no style is specified, use the one standard in your discipline:

Social sciences (psychology, education, sociology, nursing, business) → APA
Humanities (literature, languages, philosophy, cultural studies) → MLA
History, arts, and publishing → Chicago

The most important thing is not which style you choose — it is that you apply it consistently throughout your paper. Mixing styles is worse than using any single one imperfectly.
