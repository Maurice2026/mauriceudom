The Difference Between British English and Nigerian English: What Students Need to Know

When Nigerian English is perfectly natural — and when standard British English is the only acceptable choice.

Nigerian English is not broken English. It is a legitimate variety of the English language, shaped by the cultures, languages, and social realities of over 200 million people. It has its own vocabulary, expressions, and grammatical tendencies that are understood and accepted across the country.

But here is the challenge every Nigerian student faces: in academic writing, professional communication, and standardised examinations like WAEC, JAMB, and IELTS, the standard expected is British English. If you do not understand the differences between the two, you will lose marks without knowing why.

This article maps out the key differences — in vocabulary, grammar, expressions, and idioms — so that you can move confidently between both varieties depending on the situation.

Vocabulary Differences

The most obvious differences between Nigerian English and British English are in vocabulary. Many words that Nigerians use daily are either unique to Nigerian English or carry a different meaning from their British English equivalents.

Nigerian English British English Context
Torch Flashlight / Torch Both are acceptable; "torch" is actually British
Minerals Soft drinks / Fizzy drinks "Minerals" for soft drinks is uniquely Nigerian
Biro Pen / Ballpoint pen "Biro" is understood in British English but less common
Nylon / Polythene bag Plastic bag "Nylon" for plastic bag is uniquely Nigerian
Opportuned Privileged / Had the opportunity "Opportuned" does not exist in standard English
Disvirgin Deflower / Lose one's virginity "Disvirgin" is a Nigerian coinage, not standard
Trafficate Indicate / Signal "Trafficate" is not a standard English word
Off the light Turn off / Switch off the light "Off" is not used as a verb in standard English
Branch Stop by / Drop in "Let me branch at your house" is Nigerian usage
Next tomorrow The day after tomorrow "Next tomorrow" is not standard English
Important: Words like "opportuned," "trafficate," and "disvirgin" are Nigerian coinages — they do not exist in any standard English dictionary. Using them in formal writing, examinations, or international communication will be marked as errors. Always check whether a word you use regularly is actually in the dictionary.

Grammar Differences

Nigerian English has developed certain grammatical patterns that differ from standard British English. Most of these come from the influence of Nigerian languages — Yoruba, Igbo, Hausa, Edo, Ibibio, and others — on English sentence structure.

1. Using "on" and "off" as verbs

In Nigerian English, "on" and "off" are commonly used as verbs: "On the generator," "Off the television." In standard British English, these are not verbs. You must say "turn on" or "switch off."

Nigerian English
Please on the fan.
Standard English
Please turn on the fan.
2. Dropping articles ("a," "an," "the")

Many Nigerian languages do not have articles. This leads to their frequent omission in Nigerian English, especially before countable nouns.

Nigerian English
He is lecturer in my department.
Standard English
He is a lecturer in my department.
3. Pluralising uncountable nouns

Certain nouns that are uncountable in British English are routinely pluralised in Nigerian English. Words like "equipment," "information," "furniture," "advice," and "luggage" have no plural form in standard English.

Nigerian English
We need more equipments and furnitures.
Standard English
We need more equipment and furniture.

Other commonly over-pluralised words in Nigerian English include "informations" (information), "advices" (advice), "staffs" (staff, when referring to personnel), and "luggages" (luggage).

4. Wrong preposition usage

Preposition choice in English is often idiomatic — it cannot be logically deduced and must be learned. Mother tongue interference causes Nigerian students to attach prepositions that feel natural in their local language but are incorrect in English.

Nigerian English
She discussed about the project.
Standard English
She discussed the project.
Nigerian English
He requested for a transfer.
Standard English
He requested a transfer.
5. Using continuous tense for states

In Nigerian English, the present continuous tense is often used for verbs that describe states rather than actions. In standard English, stative verbs — words like "know," "believe," "understand," "belong," and "have" (possession) — are not used in the continuous form.

Nigerian English
I am having a car. / She is knowing the answer.
Standard English
I have a car. / She knows the answer.

Expressions and Idioms

Nigerian English has developed its own set of idiomatic expressions that are perfectly understood within Nigeria but would confuse a British or American speaker. Here are some of the most common ones with their standard English equivalents.

Expressions unique to Nigerian English
Nigerian
"I will flash you." (I will give you a missed call.)
Standard
"I will give you a missed call."
Nigerian
"The food is somehow." (The food tastes odd.)
Standard
"The food tastes a bit strange."
Nigerian
"He is forming." (He is pretending / showing off.)
Standard
"He is pretending / putting on airs."
Nigerian
"I'm managing." (I'm getting by / coping.)
Standard
"I'm getting by."
Nigerian
"She put to bed." (She gave birth.)
Standard
"She gave birth / had a baby."

These expressions are colourful, efficient, and deeply embedded in Nigerian culture. There is nothing inferior about them. But you need to know when they are appropriate and when they are not.

When to Use Which

Use standard British English when:

Writing academic essays, dissertations, and research papers
Sitting for standardised examinations (WAEC, JAMB, IELTS, TOEFL)
Writing formal letters, applications, and professional reports
Publishing in journals or writing for an international audience
Communicating in official or legal contexts

Nigerian English is perfectly acceptable when:

Speaking or writing informally with fellow Nigerians
Writing dialogue in fiction, drama, or screenplays set in Nigeria
Writing for a Nigerian audience in blogs, social media, or journalism (where the context allows it)
Everyday conversation

Is Nigerian English "Wrong"?

No. Nigerian English is a variety of English, just as American English, Australian English, and Indian English are varieties. Each has its own features shaped by local culture and languages. Linguists do not rank one variety above another — they are simply different.

However, in academic and formal contexts, there is a shared standard — and in Nigeria, that standard is British English. Knowing the difference between the two is not about abandoning your identity. It is about having the versatility to communicate effectively in every situation. A writer who can move fluently between Nigerian English and standard British English is more powerful than one who can use only one.

Think of it this way: you do not speak to your professor the same way you speak to your friends at a beer parlour. The language you use changes with the context. The same applies to writing. Master both registers, and you will never be caught off-guard.

Practical tip: When in doubt about whether a word or expression is standard English, check a reputable dictionary such as the Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary. If the word does not appear there, it is likely a Nigerianism and should be avoided in formal writing.
