My Writing Process: How I Go from Idea to Published Book

A behind-the-scenes look at how seven books were written — and what aspiring writers can learn from the journey.

People often ask me: "How do you write a book?" They expect a secret — some technique or trick that makes the process easier. The honest answer is that there is no trick. There is only a process, and the willingness to show up for it day after day, even when the words do not come easily.

Over seven published works — spanning tragedy, comedy, satire, and academic scholarship — I have developed a process that works for me. It is not the only way to write. But if you are an aspiring author wondering how a book actually gets made, this is how it happens in my world.

01
The Spark — Where Ideas Come From

Every book begins with a question, an image, or an anger. The Blind Gods began with anger — at the systemic injustice I witnessed against women who had no voice and no recourse. The Golden Home began with a character — a graduate reduced to working as a gatekeeper, whose intelligence and ambition turned toward schemes instead of service. I saw that man. He lived in my neighbourhood of thought long before he lived on the page.

Ideas do not arrive fully formed. They arrive as fragments — a conversation overheard, a news headline that haunts you, a question you cannot answer. The writer's job is to recognise these fragments and hold onto them. I keep a notebook. When something strikes me — a phrase, a situation, a character trait — I write it down immediately. Most entries lead nowhere. But occasionally, one refuses to leave me alone. That is the one I pursue.

02
Research — Understanding Before Writing

Before I write a single line of dialogue or a single paragraph of argument, I research. For my plays, this means understanding the social, cultural, and historical context of the story — the communities, the power structures, the language patterns, the rituals. For my academic books, it means reading extensively across the existing scholarship to understand what has been said and what still needs to be said.

Research is not just about facts. It is about empathy. To write a convincing character, you must understand how they think, what they fear, and what they believe they deserve. To write a convincing academic argument, you must understand the strongest version of the position you disagree with. Research is the foundation of honest writing.

03
Outlining — Building the Architecture

I do not write from beginning to end without a plan. I outline. For a play, this means mapping the dramatic arc — the inciting incident, the rising action, the climax, the resolution. I know the ending before I write the beginning. This does not mean I follow the outline rigidly; characters often surprise me and the story shifts. But the outline ensures I always know where I am going, even when I take a detour.

For academic works, the outline is even more essential. Each chapter must build logically on the one before it. Each section must serve the overall argument. I outline down to the paragraph level — what will each paragraph prove, and what evidence will it use?

04
The First Draft — Getting It Down

The first draft is the hardest and the ugliest. Its only job is to exist. I write the first draft quickly, without stopping to perfect sentences or second-guess word choices. If I get stuck on a scene, I skip it and come back later. The goal is momentum — to move from blank page to completed manuscript as fast as possible.

"The first draft is just you telling yourself the story." — Terry Pratchett

This is the phase where most aspiring writers give up. They write three chapters, reread them, decide they are terrible, and stop. The secret is to keep writing anyway. Every published book was once a terrible first draft. The magic happens in revision.

05
Revision — Where the Real Writing Happens

I revise every manuscript at least three times. The first revision focuses on structure — does the argument hold? Does the plot work? Are there scenes or chapters that need to be cut, moved, or expanded? The second revision focuses on language — sentence clarity, word choice, rhythm, and dialogue. The third revision focuses on details — grammar, punctuation, consistency, and formatting.

Revision is not punishment. It is craftsmanship. A carpenter does not build a table in one pass. Neither does a writer build a book. I have cut entire chapters that I spent weeks writing because they did not serve the story. It hurts. But the book is always better for it.

06
External Feedback — The Trusted Reader

After my own revisions, I give the manuscript to two or three trusted readers — people whose literary judgement I respect and who will tell me the truth, not what I want to hear. Their feedback reveals blind spots I cannot see myself. A scene I thought was powerful may confuse them. A character I considered minor may fascinate them. These insights reshape the final draft in ways I could never achieve alone.

For academic works, this stage often involves peer review — colleagues in the field who can verify the accuracy and strength of the argument.

07
Professional Editing and Publication

The final stage is professional editing — a fresh, expert pair of eyes that catches what everyone else has missed. Then formatting, cover design, and the decision of how and where to publish. I have published through both traditional publishers and self-publishing platforms, and each has its advantages depending on the work and the audience.

Publication is not the end. It is the beginning of a new conversation — between the book and its readers. And by the time that conversation starts, I am usually already deep into the research for the next book.

What I wish I had known when I started:

Writing is rewriting. The first draft is not the book — it is the raw material from which the book will be carved.

Discipline matters more than inspiration. I write on a schedule, not when I "feel like it." Waiting for inspiration is a luxury that published authors cannot afford.

Every book teaches you something the previous one did not. Your seventh book will be better than your first — not because you have more talent, but because you have more craft.

The hardest part is not writing. The hardest part is the willingness to be honest on the page — to write what is true, not what is comfortable.
