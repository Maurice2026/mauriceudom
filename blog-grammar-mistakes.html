20 Common Grammar Mistakes Nigerian Students Make (And How to Fix Them)

A practical guide for university students, WAEC and JAMB candidates, and anyone who wants to write better English.

Every year, thousands of Nigerian students lose marks in examinations and academic papers — not because they lack ideas, but because avoidable grammar errors weaken their writing. Many of these mistakes stem from the influence of Nigerian Pidgin, mother tongue interference, and habits that go uncorrected through years of schooling.

The good news is that these errors are predictable. Once you see the pattern, you can fix it. This article identifies the twenty most common grammar mistakes I have encountered across decades of teaching English in Nigerian universities and polytechnics — with clear explanations and corrected examples for each.

Subject-Verb Agreement Errors

Mistake 01
Using plural verbs with singular subjects

This is the single most common error in Nigerian student writing. When the subject of a sentence is singular, the verb must also be singular — regardless of what comes between them.

Incorrect
The list of candidates have been released.
Correct
The list of candidates has been released.

The subject is "list" (singular), not "candidates." The phrase "of candidates" is a prepositional phrase that modifies the subject — it does not change the verb.

Mistake 02
Errors with "everybody," "everyone," and "nobody"

Words like everybody, everyone, nobody, somebody, and each are singular. They always take singular verbs and singular pronouns.

Incorrect
Everybody are expected to submit their assignment.
Correct
Everybody is expected to submit his or her assignment.
Mistake 03
Confusing "a number of" with "the number of"

"A number of" is plural and takes a plural verb. "The number of" is singular and takes a singular verb. This distinction trips up even advanced students.

Incorrect
A number of students has failed the course.
Correct
A number of students have failed the course.
Also Correct
The number of students who failed is alarming.

Tense Errors

Mistake 04
Mixing past and present tenses in the same sentence

Many students switch tenses mid-sentence or mid-paragraph without reason. Once you choose a tense, stay with it unless the meaning requires a change.

Incorrect
He went to the market and buys some rice.
Correct
He went to the market and bought some rice.
Mistake 05
Using "did" with the past tense instead of the base form

When you use "did" as a helping verb, the main verb must return to its base (present) form. This is one of the most frequent errors in spoken and written Nigerian English.

Incorrect
She did not went to class yesterday.
Correct
She did not go to class yesterday.
Mistake 06
Incorrect use of the present perfect tense

The present perfect ("has/have + past participle") is used for actions that started in the past and continue to the present, or for past actions with present relevance. Nigerian students often confuse it with the simple past.

Incorrect
I have saw that film before.
Correct
I have seen that film before.

Preposition Errors

Mistake 07
Using wrong prepositions after verbs

Preposition usage in English is largely idiomatic — it must be learned verb by verb. Mother tongue interference often leads Nigerian students to attach the wrong preposition to common verbs.

Incorrect
She is married to three children. / He congratulated me for my success.
Correct
She is married with three children. / He congratulated me on my success.
Mistake 08
Adding unnecessary prepositions

Some verbs in English do not take prepositions at all. Adding one where none is needed is a common error influenced by Nigerian Pidgin and local language patterns.

Incorrect
He entered into the room. / She discussed about the matter.
Correct
He entered the room. / She discussed the matter.

Other examples: "emphasise" (not "emphasise on"), "request" (not "request for"), and "comprise" (not "comprise of").

Word Choice and Vocabulary Errors

Mistake 09
Confusing "its" and "it's"

This small error appears in almost every batch of student essays. "It's" is a contraction of "it is" or "it has." "Its" (no apostrophe) is the possessive form.

Incorrect
The university has lost it's reputation.
Correct
The university has lost its reputation.
Mistake 10
Confusing "your" and "you're"

Like the previous error, this comes from confusing contractions with possessives. "You're" means "you are." "Your" indicates possession.

Incorrect
Your the best candidate for the position.
Correct
You're the best candidate for the position.
Mistake 11
Using "am" instead of "I'm" or "I am"

In informal Nigerian English, many people write "am" when they mean "I am" or "I'm." In formal writing, this is incorrect — "am" is a verb and cannot stand alone as a subject.

Incorrect
Am coming to the lecture today.
Correct
I am coming to the lecture today.
Mistake 12
Misusing "lose" and "loose"

"Lose" is a verb meaning to misplace or to fail to win. "Loose" is an adjective meaning not tight. These are two completely different words, yet they are frequently swapped.

Incorrect
I don't want to loose my scholarship.
Correct
I don't want to lose my scholarship.

Sentence Structure Errors

Mistake 13
Run-on sentences and comma splices

A run-on sentence joins two independent clauses without proper punctuation or a conjunction. A comma splice uses only a comma to connect them. Both are serious errors in formal writing.

Incorrect
The lecturer cancelled the class, the students went home.
Correct
The lecturer cancelled the class, so the students went home.

You can also fix this by using a full stop, a semicolon, or a conjunction such as "and," "but," or "therefore."

Mistake 14
Sentence fragments

A sentence fragment is an incomplete sentence — it lacks a subject, a verb, or a complete thought. Students often write fragments when they break a sentence into two parts unnecessarily.

Incorrect
The government introduced new policies. Which affected the entire education sector.
Correct
The government introduced new policies which affected the entire education sector.
Mistake 15
Double negatives

In standard English, two negatives in the same clause cancel each other out and create a positive meaning. This is a frequent error influenced by Pidgin English.

Incorrect
I don't know nothing about the exam.
Correct
I don't know anything about the exam.

Commonly Misused Expressions

Mistake 16
"Cope with" not "cope up with"

The correct expression in standard English is "cope with." The addition of "up" is a common Nigerian English error with no basis in standard usage.

Incorrect
Students must learn to cope up with pressure.
Correct
Students must learn to cope with pressure.
Mistake 17
"Pick up a call" not "pick a call"

While "pick a call" is widely understood in Nigeria, the standard English expression is "pick up a call" or, more formally, "answer a call."

Incorrect
She refused to pick my call.
Correct
She refused to pick up my call / answer my call.
Mistake 18
"According to" not "according to me"

"According to" is used to cite someone else's view or an external source. You do not use it to introduce your own opinion — that would be "in my opinion" or "I believe."

Incorrect
According to me, the policy is unfair.
Correct
In my opinion, the policy is unfair.
Mistake 19
"Pass an exam" not "pass out of an exam"

In standard English, "pass out" means to faint or lose consciousness. The correct expression for succeeding in an examination is simply "pass."

Incorrect
I want to pass out with a first class.
Correct
I want to graduate with a first class.
Mistake 20
"At the end of the day" used as filler

This expression has become a meaningless filler in Nigerian English, inserted into every other sentence. In good writing, every phrase must earn its place. Use it only when you genuinely mean "ultimately" or "when everything is considered" — and even then, sparingly.

Incorrect
At the end of the day, education is important. At the end of the day, we need to study hard.
Correct
Ultimately, education is what matters most.

How to Improve

Five habits that will strengthen your grammar:

1. Read widely. The more standard English you absorb through books, newspapers, and academic texts, the more natural correct grammar becomes.

2. Proofread everything. Never submit any piece of writing without reading it through at least twice. Most of the errors listed above are caught on a second reading.

3. Learn the rules, not just the corrections. Understanding why something is wrong is more powerful than memorising the right answer.

4. Keep an error log. Write down every grammar mistake your lecturers mark in your work. Review the list before your next assignment.

5. Use a good reference text. A comprehensive grammar guide — such as Reader's Companion on Use of English — is an invaluable companion throughout your studies.

Grammar is not about perfection — it is about clarity. The purpose of correct grammar is to ensure that your reader understands exactly what you mean, without confusion or distraction. Every error you eliminate makes your writing stronger, your arguments more persuasive, and your academic work more credible.

Master these twenty mistakes, and you will write better than the majority of graduates in Nigeria today. That is not an exaggeration — it is a fact born of thirty years in the classroom.
