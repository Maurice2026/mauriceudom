Characterisation as an Asset to Novel Writing

April 26, 2026  |  12 min read

Every novel you've ever loved — every story that made you miss your bus stop or stay up past midnight — owes a debt to one thing: characterisation. Plot may be the skeleton of a story, but characterisation is the beating heart. Without it, even the most inventive storyline reads like a Wikipedia summary. With it, readers don't just follow a story — they feel it.

Let's explore why characterisation is one of the most powerful tools in a novelist's kit, and how to wield it well.

What Is Characterisation?

Characterisation is the craft of bringing a fictional person to life on the page. It's how a writer reveals who a character is — their personality, desires, fears, contradictions, and quirks — through deliberate storytelling choices.

There are two kinds. Direct characterisation is when the author simply tells you something about a character: "Amara was stubborn to her core." Indirect characterisation is subtler and far more engaging — it's when you learn about a character through their actions, dialogue, thoughts, and how other characters react to them.

Think of it this way: direct characterisation hands you a label. Indirect characterisation lets you peel back the layers yourself.

• • •

Characterisation in Action: The Appetiser

Before we go deep, let's start with a small taste of what indirect characterisation looks like in practice. Consider this brief passage:

Tunde sat at the kitchen table, lining up his pens by colour. He did this every morning before writing — blue, then black, then red. When his sister knocked one out of line reaching for the sugar, he stopped mid-sentence, replaced the pen, and started his paragraph over from the beginning.

Notice what just happened. Not a single line told you Tunde is meticulous, ritualistic, or perhaps anxious. But you know it now. You felt it through his small, deliberate actions. That's the magic of strong characterisation — it shows rather than tells, and it trusts the reader to draw conclusions.

Now, let's see what happens when a writer takes this principle further — building a full portrait where every physical detail earns its place in the story.

• • •

The Body Tells a Story Too: Physical Characterisation

Characterisation isn't only about personality and behaviour — it also includes the physical. A character's body, appearance, and physical abilities are powerful storytelling tools that shape how they move through the world and how the world responds to them.

When an author describes a character's height, build, scars, injuries, or physical limitations, they aren't just painting a picture for the reader's imagination. They're laying the groundwork for conflict, vulnerability, and identity.

Consider this example:

Etim stood six feet tall, towering over most of his classmates, but there was nothing imposing about him. His frame was lean — almost fragile — the kind of body that seemed built for library chairs, not football fields. A cast wrapped his broken arm from wrist to elbow, and he carried it close to his chest like a secret he wished people would stop noticing.

His eyes were his most striking feature — deep-set and watchful, the kind that lingered on a page or a person a moment longer than was comfortable. They gave him an air of knowing, as though he had already read the ending of whatever situation he walked into. He kept his hair cropped low, neat and deliberate, the sharp lines of his haircut the one thing about his appearance that suggested he cared how the world saw him.

Etim walked slowly, with a scholar's patience — unhurried, slightly stooped, as if his height embarrassed him and he was always trying to meet the world halfway. He never rushed, even when he was late. And when he spoke, it was the same way: measured, precise, every word chosen like a move in a chess game. He didn't raise his voice. He never needed to. In the lecture hall, one quiet answer from Etim could silence a room full of noise. The professors admired him. The studious ones among his classmates respected him. Some even resented him — the way he could unravel a problem in seconds that left others sweating for an hour.

But admiration has its limits. When the boys behind the hostel settled their arguments with fists, Etim always found a reason to walk the other way. His height gave him presence, but his thin arms and careful stride told the truth — he was not a fighter. The same hands that wrote the most brilliant answers in the examination hall trembled when someone shoved past him in the corridor. The girls called him "fine boy with sense." The rougher boys called him other things. Etim heard them all. His watchful eyes missed nothing. But he had learned long ago that not every battle is won standing up — and that the ones worth fighting rarely involve fists.

Look at how much work this passage is doing. Every physical detail is connected to who Etim is and how he moves through the world:

His eyes — deep-set and watchful — establish him as someone who observes and calculates. This links directly to his actions in the classroom, where his quiet intelligence dominates. His low-cropped hair, neat and deliberate, reveals that he is not careless about himself — he simply chooses where to invest his pride. His lean physique and the broken arm create a visual contrast between his towering height and his actual vulnerability, setting up the central tension: a man who looks like he should be powerful but whose power lives entirely in his mind.

Then there is how he walks — slowly, slightly stooped, unhurried. This isn't just physical description; it mirrors his personality. He is patient, thoughtful, never reactive. And his speech — measured, precise, never loud — reinforces the same idea. Etim doesn't dominate a room with volume. He dominates it with clarity.

Finally, notice how the world responds to him. The professors admire him. The studious classmates respect him. Some resent him. The girls find him attractive and intelligent. The rougher boys see weakness. Etim is not universally loved or universally hated — he is perceived differently depending on who is looking, which is exactly how real people work. And each of these reactions connects back to his physical and intellectual traits, creating a web of relationships that the plot can pull on.

This is the power of full-bodied physical characterisation. It's not a passport description — height, weight, eye colour. It's a living portrait where every detail earns its place by shaping action, perception, and story.

Characterisation Must Link with the Storyline

A well-drawn character is not a portrait hanging on a wall. They need to be woven into the fabric of the story. Their traits should matter — not just as decoration, but as forces that shape the events of the narrative.

If your character is reckless, that recklessness should cause something to happen. If your character is deeply loyal, that loyalty should be tested. Characterisation and storyline should feed each other in a continuous loop: the character's nature drives their choices, and those choices push the story forward.

A character who is brave but whose bravery never matters to the plot is just a character sheet — not a living participant in the story.

• • •

Consistency: The Trust Between Writer and Reader

Once you establish who a character is, the reader begins to trust that portrait. They build expectations. They start to know your character the way they know a friend. Breaking that trust without reason is one of the fastest ways to lose a reader.

This doesn't mean characters can't change — growth is the soul of storytelling. But change must be earned. A timid character who suddenly delivers a fearless speech in Chapter 12 needs a journey that makes that moment feel inevitable, not random.

Consistency means that a character's voice, habits, values, and reactions remain recognisable throughout the story, even as circumstances push them to evolve. If your character hates dishonesty in Chapter 3, they shouldn't casually lie in Chapter 15 without a compelling reason and visible internal conflict.

At the heart of this is something deeper: desire versus fear. Every well-drawn character wants something — and is simultaneously afraid of something. These two forces pull in opposite directions and create the internal tension that makes a character feel alive. Etim desires respect and intellectual recognition, but he fears physical confrontation and exposure of his vulnerability. These competing forces don't cancel each other out — they create a character who feels torn, layered, and human. When a character's desire and fear remain consistent, even their surprising choices feel earned. When those forces shift, the writer must show us why.

• • •

Characterisation and Plot: Two Sides of the Same Coin

The best novels make it impossible to separate character from plot. One creates the other.

Take Chinua Achebe's Things Fall Apart. Okonkwo's entire tragedy is built on characterisation. He is proud, fierce, and terrified of appearing weak — traits rooted in his shame over his father's failures. These traits don't just describe him; they are the plot. His fear of weakness drives him to reject gentleness, alienate his son, and ultimately resist change with a rigidity that destroys him.

Remove Okonkwo's characterisation, and the plot of Things Fall Apart collapses. There is no story without who he is. That is the gold standard — when a character's inner world is so tightly bound to the external events that neither can exist without the other.

• • •

Every Character Must Stand on Their Own

One of the clearest signs of skilled writing is distinction between characters. If you cover the names in your dialogue and every character sounds the same, you have a characterisation problem.

Each character in your novel should have their own voice, worldview, and way of moving through the story. This doesn't mean giving everyone a quirky catchphrase. It means understanding that people are shaped differently by their experiences, and that those differences show up in how they speak, what they notice, what they avoid, and what they want.

In Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie's Half of a Yellow Sun, consider how different Olanna, Ugwu, and Richard are from one another. Olanna is graceful and idealistic. Ugwu is observant, eager, and navigating a world far bigger than his village prepared him for. Richard is an outsider, earnest but always slightly out of step. Each one could carry a novel on their own — and yet together, they create a story richer than any single perspective could deliver.

When your characters are truly distinct, they create natural tension simply by being in the same room. Their differences generate conflict, humour, and depth without the writer having to manufacture drama.

• • •

Final Thoughts

Characterisation is not a garnish you add to a finished plot. It is the foundation. It determines how your story moves, what it means, and whether your readers will still be thinking about it long after the final page.

So the next time you sit down to write, don't start with what happens. Start with who it happens to — and make sure that "who" is someone so vivid, so specific, so deeply imagined that the story could belong to no one else.

Happy writing — and keep creating.
