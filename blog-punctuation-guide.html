Punctuation Made Simple: Mastering the Comma, Semicolon and Colon

A clear, practical reference guide you will return to again and again — with rules and examples for every major punctuation mark.

Punctuation is the traffic system of written English. Commas tell the reader to pause. Full stops tell them to stop. Semicolons signal a connection between two ideas. Get these signals right, and your writing flows naturally. Get them wrong, and even a brilliant argument becomes difficult to follow.

The problem is that most students were never properly taught the rules. They scatter commas like seasoning — a pinch here, a handful there — and hope for the best. This article gives you the actual rules, clearly explained, so you never have to guess again.

,
The Comma

The comma is the most used — and most misused — punctuation mark in English. It has specific rules. Here are the ones that matter most.

Rule 1: Use a comma before a coordinating conjunction joining two independent clauses

The seven coordinating conjunctions are: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so (remember them as FANBOYS). When they connect two complete sentences, put a comma before them.

Correct
She studied all night, but she still failed the exam.

Both "She studied all night" and "she still failed the exam" are complete sentences. The comma is required before "but."

Rule 2: Use commas to separate items in a list

When you list three or more items, separate them with commas. The comma before the final "and" (called the Oxford comma) is optional in British English but recommended for clarity.

Correct
She packed her books, laptop, and stationery.
Rule 3: Use commas after introductory elements

When a sentence begins with a word, phrase, or clause that comes before the main subject and verb, follow it with a comma.

Correct
After the lecture, the students gathered in the cafeteria.
Correct
However, the results were inconclusive.
Rule 4: Use commas to set off non-essential information

If a word, phrase, or clause can be removed from the sentence without changing its core meaning, enclose it in commas.

Correct
Professor Adekunle, who teaches linguistics, has published twelve papers.

The clause "who teaches linguistics" is additional information — the sentence works without it. So it gets commas.

Rule 5: Do NOT put a comma between a subject and its verb

This is one of the most common comma errors in Nigerian student writing.

Incorrect
The man who came yesterday, is my uncle.
Correct
The man who came yesterday is my uncle.

The Semicolon

The semicolon is simpler than most people think. It has essentially two uses, and once you learn them, you will wonder why you ever found it confusing.

Rule 1: Use a semicolon to join two closely related independent clauses

When two complete sentences are so closely connected in meaning that a full stop feels like too strong a break, use a semicolon instead.

Correct
The exam was difficult; most students failed.

Both halves are complete sentences. The semicolon tells the reader: these two ideas are directly connected. You could use a full stop instead, but the semicolon is more elegant here because the second sentence is a direct consequence of the first.

Rule 2: Use a semicolon before conjunctive adverbs

Words like however, therefore, moreover, furthermore, consequently, nevertheless, and meanwhile are not conjunctions — they are adverbs. When they join two independent clauses, use a semicolon before them and a comma after.

Incorrect
The roads were flooded, however, the students came to class.
Correct
The roads were flooded; however, the students came to class.
The key difference: A comma + conjunction (and, but, so) joins two clauses. A semicolon replaces the conjunction entirely, or pairs with a conjunctive adverb (however, therefore). Never use a comma where a semicolon is needed — that error is called a comma splice.

The Colon

The colon is an announcement mark. It says to the reader: what follows explains, expands, or illustrates what came before.

Rule 1: Use a colon to introduce a list

The clause before the colon must be a complete sentence.

Incorrect
The ingredients include: flour, sugar, and butter.
Correct
The recipe requires three ingredients: flour, sugar, and butter.

In the incorrect version, "The ingredients include" is not a complete sentence (the verb "include" needs its object). In the correct version, "The recipe requires three ingredients" stands on its own.

Rule 2: Use a colon to introduce an explanation or elaboration

When the second part of a sentence explains or amplifies the first, a colon is the right choice.

Correct
The reason was simple: nobody had studied.
Correct
He had one goal: to graduate with a first class.
'
The Apostrophe

The apostrophe has only two functions in English: to show possession and to indicate missing letters in contractions. It is never used to form plurals.

Rule 1: Possession — singular nouns

Add 's to a singular noun to show ownership, even if the noun already ends in s.

Correct
The student's book was on the table. / James's presentation was excellent.
Rule 2: Possession — plural nouns

If the plural noun ends in s, add only an apostrophe. If it does not end in s, add 's.

Correct
The students' results were published. / The children's playground was renovated.
Rule 3: Never use an apostrophe to form a plural

This is one of the most widespread errors in Nigeria — on signboards, menus, flyers, and student essays alike.

Incorrect
Fresh tomato's for sale. / All lecturer's should attend.
Correct
Fresh tomatoes for sale. / All lecturers should attend.
.
The Full Stop (Period)

The full stop seems obvious, but two errors involving it are surprisingly common among students.

Do not use a full stop after a question or exclamation

If the sentence is a question, use a question mark. If it expresses strong emotion, use an exclamation mark. Never double up.

Incorrect
What time is the exam. / That was incredible.!
Correct
What time is the exam? / That was incredible!
Use full stops to avoid run-on sentences

If you find yourself writing sentences that stretch beyond three lines, there is almost certainly a place where a full stop should go. Long sentences are not a sign of sophistication — they are usually a sign of poor punctuation.

Too Long
The government introduced a new policy that was meant to reform the education sector but many people disagreed with it because they felt it was poorly implemented and did not address the real problems facing universities in the country.
Better
The government introduced a new policy to reform the education sector. However, many people disagreed with it. They felt it was poorly implemented and failed to address the real problems facing universities.

Final Advice

Three habits that will transform your punctuation:

1. Read your work aloud. Your voice naturally pauses where commas should go and stops where full stops belong. If you run out of breath in a single sentence, it is too long.

2. When in doubt, use a full stop. A short, clear sentence is always better than a long, confused one. If you are unsure whether to use a comma, semicolon, or colon, break the sentence into two. Clarity comes first.

3. Study good writing. Pay attention to how published authors and journalists use punctuation. You will begin to absorb the patterns naturally. A solid reference guide like Reader's Companion on Use of English can serve as a permanent desk companion for questions like these.

Punctuation may seem like a small detail, but it is the difference between writing that is easy to read and writing that exhausts the reader. Every mark you place correctly is a mark of respect for your reader's time and intelligence. Master these rules, and your writing will carry a quiet authority that sets it apart.
