Why Every Writer Should Read African Fiction

By Maurice Udom  |  April 2026  |  6 min read

As writers, we are always searching for new voices, new perspectives, and new ways of telling the human story. If you have not yet explored African fiction, you are missing one of the richest and most powerful literary traditions in the world — one that will transform how you write, how you think, and how you see the world.

Writers grow by reading widely. We all know this. Yet so many writers limit themselves to the literature of their own region or culture — and in doing so, they cut themselves off from an entire universe of storytelling wisdom. African fiction offers something truly unique: stories rooted in ancient oral traditions, rich cultural complexity, and the kind of raw human truth that only comes from writers who have lived deeply.

Africa and India — two great storytelling civilisations

India and Africa share something profound. Both are ancient civilisations with deep traditions of oral storytelling that long predate the written word. Both have produced literature that grapples with colonialism, identity, family, and the search for belonging in a rapidly changing world. Both have writers who are now reshaping global literature on their own terms.

When an Indian writer reads African fiction, they are not reading something foreign. They are reading something familiar — stories about community, about the weight of tradition, about the collision between the old world and the new. The themes resonate deeply because the human experiences are shared, even if the settings and cultures are different.

"The best literature does not just tell you about another world — it shows you something true about your own. African fiction does exactly that for every reader, everywhere in the world."

What African fiction teaches writers

Reading African fiction will make you a better writer in specific, practical ways. African storytelling has always understood that a story is not just a sequence of events — it is a living thing, connected to community, to ancestors, to the land. This understanding produces fiction with a depth and resonance that is hard to find elsewhere.

What you will learn from African fiction as a writer:

  • How to weave oral storytelling techniques into written fiction
  • How to give voice to community and collective experience, not just the individual
  • How to handle themes of identity, belonging and cultural conflict with power and grace
  • How to write characters who carry the full weight of their history and culture
  • How to find the universal in the deeply specific and local

Nigerian fiction — a world of its own

Nigeria in particular has produced a literary tradition of extraordinary richness. With over 500 languages, a history of kingdoms and empires, and a storytelling tradition that stretches back thousands of years, Nigeria offers fiction writers a world of almost unlimited creative material. Nigerian stories deal with power, loyalty, betrayal, love, war, and the supernatural — all rooted in a cultural reality that is unique and deeply compelling.

My own novels are written in this tradition. They draw on the mythology, history, and lived experience of Nigerian culture to tell stories that speak to universal human themes — the struggle for power, the meaning of identity, the conflict between tradition and change. These are stories that writers everywhere will recognise and respond to, because they are about what it means to be human.

Two books every writer should read

I would like to invite you to explore two of my novels. Both are rooted in Nigerian culture and history, and both offer the kind of rich, layered storytelling that will inspire and challenge you as a writer.

The Blind Gods — a powerful story of identity, mystery, and the hidden forces that shape our lives. This novel draws on Nigerian mythology and the ancient belief systems that lie beneath the surface of modern life. As a writer, you will find its narrative structure and its handling of the supernatural deeply instructive.

The Broken Sceptre — a story of power, betrayal, and the fate of a kingdom. Set against the backdrop of Nigerian royal history and politics, this novel explores what happens when the symbols of authority are shattered — and what people are willing to do to restore them.

"Reading outside your own culture is not just an act of curiosity — it is an act of craft. The writer who reads widely writes deeply."

An invitation to fellow writers

I write this as a fellow lover of words and stories. I know what it means to sit with a blank page and search for the right way to tell a truth. I know what it means to read a book that changes the way you see your own writing. African fiction has that power — and I believe that writers from every part of the world, including the rich and vibrant writing community of India, will find in it something that enriches and deepens their own creative work.

I warmly invite you to explore my books. They are available online and can be delivered anywhere in the world. Read them as a reader, read them as a writer — and let me know what you think.
