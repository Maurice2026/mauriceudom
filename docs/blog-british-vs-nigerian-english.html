How Local English Differs from Standard English: A Guide for ESL Learners Worldwide

When your local English is perfectly natural — and when standard British or American English is the only acceptable choice.

English is spoken by nearly two billion people worldwide, and no two countries speak it the same way. Nigerian English, Indian English, Singaporean English, Filipino English, Ghanaian English — each is a legitimate variety shaped by local cultures, languages, and social realities.

But here is the challenge every ESL learner faces: in academic writing, professional communication, and standardised examinations like IELTS, TOEFL, Cambridge, and university entrance exams, the standard expected is usually British or American English. If you do not understand how your local English differs from the standard, you may lose marks without knowing why.

This article maps out the most common differences — in vocabulary, grammar, and expressions — drawn from English varieties across Africa, Asia, and beyond. The goal is to help you move confidently between your local variety and standard English depending on the situation.

Vocabulary Differences Around the World

Every region has words and expressions that are perfectly understood locally but would confuse a British or American speaker. Here are examples from several major English-speaking communities.

Local Expression Standard English Region
Minerals Soft drinks / Fizzy drinks Nigeria
Prepone Move to an earlier date / Bring forward India
Chope Reserve / Save (a seat) Singapore
Comfort room (CR) Toilet / Restroom / Bathroom Philippines
Trafficate Indicate / Use turn signal Nigeria
Mugging Studying hard / Cramming India / Singapore
Off the light Turn off / Switch off the light West Africa
Rubber shoes Sneakers / Trainers Philippines
Next tomorrow The day after tomorrow West Africa
Do the needful Do what is necessary / Take the required action India
Important: Some local expressions — like "prepone" (India), "trafficate" (Nigeria), or "chope" (Singapore) — are not found in any standard English dictionary. Using them in formal writing, examinations, or international communication will be marked as errors. Always verify whether a word you use regularly exists in a standard dictionary like the Oxford or Cambridge.

Grammar Differences

Local English varieties often develop grammatical patterns that differ from standard British or American English. Most of these come from the influence of mother tongue languages on English sentence structure. Here are the most common patterns seen across multiple regions.

1. Using "on" and "off" as verbs

West Africa South Asia

In many local varieties, "on" and "off" are used as verbs: "On the generator," "Off the television." In standard English, these are not verbs. You must say "turn on" or "switch off."

Local English
Please on the fan.
Standard English
Please turn on the fan.
2. Dropping articles ("a," "an," "the")

East Asia West Africa South Asia

Many languages worldwide — including Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Yoruba, Igbo, Hindi, and Tagalog — do not have articles. This leads to their frequent omission in local English, especially before countable nouns.

Local English
He is lecturer in my department.
Standard English
He is a lecturer in my department.
3. Pluralising uncountable nouns

Africa South Asia Southeast Asia

Certain nouns that are uncountable in standard English are routinely pluralised in local varieties. Words like "equipment," "information," "furniture," "advice," and "luggage" have no plural form in standard English.

Local English
We need more equipments and furnitures.
Standard English
We need more equipment and furniture.

Other commonly over-pluralised words include "informations" (information), "advices" (advice), "staffs" (staff, when referring to personnel), and "luggages" (luggage).

4. Wrong preposition usage

Global ESL

Preposition choice in English is often idiomatic — it cannot be logically deduced and must be learned. Mother tongue interference causes learners everywhere to attach prepositions that feel natural in their local language but are incorrect in English.

Local English
She discussed about the project.
Standard English
She discussed the project.
Local English
He requested for a transfer.
Standard English
He requested a transfer.
5. Using continuous tense for states

South Asia West Africa

In several local varieties, the present continuous tense is used for verbs that describe states rather than actions. In standard English, stative verbs — words like "know," "believe," "understand," "belong," and "have" (possession) — are not used in the continuous form.

Local English
I am having a car. / She is knowing the answer.
Standard English
I have a car. / She knows the answer.

Expressions and Idioms

Every English-speaking region has developed its own idiomatic expressions that are perfectly understood locally but would puzzle speakers from other countries.

Local expressions from around the world
Nigeria
"I will flash you." (Give a missed call.)
Standard
"I will give you a missed call."
India
"Please do the needful."
Standard
"Please take the necessary action."
Singapore
"Can lah!" (Yes, it's possible.)
Standard
"Yes, that's possible."
Philippines
"She's my classmate before." (Former classmate.)
Standard
"She used to be my classmate."
West Africa
"She put to bed." (She gave birth.)
Standard
"She gave birth / had a baby."

These expressions are colourful, efficient, and deeply embedded in local culture. There is nothing inferior about them. But you need to know when they are appropriate and when they are not.

When to Use Which

Use standard British or American English when:

Writing academic essays, dissertations, and research papers
Sitting for standardised examinations (IELTS, TOEFL, Cambridge, university entrance exams)
Writing formal letters, applications, and professional reports
Publishing in journals or writing for an international audience
Communicating in official or legal contexts

Your local English variety is perfectly acceptable when:

Speaking or writing informally with people from your community
Writing dialogue in fiction, drama, or screenplays set in your region
Writing for a local audience in blogs, social media, or journalism
Everyday conversation

Is Local English "Wrong"?

Absolutely not. Nigerian English, Indian English, Singaporean English, Filipino English — each is a legitimate variety of the English language, just as American English and Australian English are varieties. Linguists do not rank one above another — they are simply different.

However, in academic and formal contexts, there is a shared international standard. Knowing the difference between your local variety and that standard is not about abandoning your identity. It is about having the versatility to communicate effectively in every situation. A writer who can move fluently between local English and standard English is more powerful than one who can use only one.

Think of it this way: you do not speak to your professor the same way you speak to your friends at a café. The language you use changes with the context. The same applies to writing. Master both registers, and you will never be caught off-guard.

Practical tip: When in doubt about whether a word or expression is standard English, check a reputable dictionary such as the Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary or the Cambridge Dictionary. If the word does not appear there, it is likely a local variety feature and should be avoided in formal writing.
