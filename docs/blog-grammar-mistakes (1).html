20 Common Grammar Mistakes Students Make (And How to Fix Them)

A practical guide for university students, ESL learners, and anyone who wants to write clearer, more accurate English.

Every year, thousands of students lose marks in examinations and academic papers — not because they lack ideas, but because avoidable grammar errors weaken their writing. Many of these mistakes stem from mother tongue interference, informal speaking habits, and patterns that go uncorrected through years of schooling.

The good news is that these errors are predictable. Whether you are studying in Lagos, London, Lahore, or Lima, the same twenty mistakes appear again and again. Once you see the pattern, you can fix it. This article identifies the most common grammar mistakes I have encountered across decades of teaching English — with clear explanations and corrected examples for each.

Subject-Verb Agreement Errors

Mistake 01
Using plural verbs with singular subjects

This is the single most common error in student writing worldwide. When the subject of a sentence is singular, the verb must also be singular — regardless of what comes between them.

Incorrect
The list of candidates have been released.
Correct
The list of candidates has been released.

The subject is "list" (singular), not "candidates." The phrase "of candidates" is a prepositional phrase that modifies the subject — it does not change the verb.

Mistake 02
Errors with "everybody," "everyone," and "nobody"

Words like everybody, everyone, nobody, somebody, and each are singular. They always take singular verbs and singular pronouns.

Incorrect
Everybody are expected to submit their assignment.
Correct
Everybody is expected to submit his or her assignment.
Mistake 03
Confusing "a number of" with "the number of"

"A number of" is plural and takes a plural verb. "The number of" is singular and takes a singular verb. This distinction trips up even advanced students.

Incorrect
A number of students has failed the course.
Correct
A number of students have failed the course.
Also Correct
The number of students who failed is alarming.

Tense Errors

Mistake 04
Mixing past and present tenses in the same sentence

Many students switch tenses mid-sentence or mid-paragraph without reason. Once you choose a tense, stay with it unless the meaning requires a change.

Incorrect
He went to the market and buys some rice.
Correct
He went to the market and bought some rice.
Mistake 05
Using "did" with the past tense instead of the base form

When you use "did" as a helping verb, the main verb must return to its base (present) form. This is one of the most frequent errors among ESL learners across Africa, Asia, and Latin America.

Incorrect
She did not went to class yesterday.
Correct
She did not go to class yesterday.
Mistake 06
Incorrect use of the present perfect tense

The present perfect ("has/have + past participle") is used for actions that started in the past and continue to the present, or for past actions with present relevance. Students often confuse it with the simple past.

Incorrect
I have saw that film before.
Correct
I have seen that film before.

Preposition Errors

Mistake 07
Using wrong prepositions after verbs

Preposition usage in English is largely idiomatic — it must be learned verb by verb. Mother tongue interference often leads students to attach the wrong preposition to common verbs, regardless of their first language.

Incorrect
He congratulated me for my success.
Correct
He congratulated me on my success.
Mistake 08
Adding unnecessary prepositions

Some verbs in English do not take prepositions at all. Adding one where none is needed is a common error influenced by local language patterns around the world.

Incorrect
He entered into the room. / She discussed about the matter.
Correct
He entered the room. / She discussed the matter.

Other examples: "emphasise" (not "emphasise on"), "request" (not "request for"), and "comprise" (not "comprise of").

Word Choice and Vocabulary Errors

Mistake 09
Confusing "its" and "it's"

This small error appears in almost every batch of student essays — from first-year undergraduates to postgraduate researchers. "It's" is a contraction of "it is" or "it has." "Its" (no apostrophe) is the possessive form.

Incorrect
The university has lost it's reputation.
Correct
The university has lost its reputation.
Mistake 10
Confusing "your" and "you're"

"You're" means "you are." "Your" indicates possession.

Incorrect
Your the best candidate for the position.
Correct
You're the best candidate for the position.
Mistake 11
Using "am" instead of "I'm" or "I am"

In many informal English-speaking communities worldwide, people write "am" when they mean "I am" or "I'm." In formal writing, "am" cannot stand alone as a subject.

Incorrect
Am coming to the lecture today.
Correct
I am coming to the lecture today.
Mistake 12
Misusing "lose" and "loose"

"Lose" is a verb meaning to misplace or to fail to win. "Loose" is an adjective meaning not tight.

Incorrect
I don't want to loose my scholarship.
Correct
I don't want to lose my scholarship.

Sentence Structure Errors

Mistake 13
Run-on sentences and comma splices

A run-on sentence joins two independent clauses without proper punctuation or a conjunction. A comma splice uses only a comma to connect them.

Incorrect
The lecturer cancelled the class, the students went home.
Correct
The lecturer cancelled the class, so the students went home.
Mistake 14
Sentence fragments

A sentence fragment is an incomplete sentence — it lacks a subject, a verb, or a complete thought.

Incorrect
The government introduced new policies. Which affected the entire education sector.
Correct
The government introduced new policies which affected the entire education sector.
Mistake 15
Double negatives

In standard English, two negatives in the same clause cancel each other out. This error is common among speakers of many languages worldwide.

Incorrect
I don't know nothing about the exam.
Correct
I don't know anything about the exam.

Commonly Misused Expressions

Mistake 16
"Cope with" not "cope up with"

The correct expression is "cope with." The addition of "up" is a widespread error found in many ESL communities across Africa and South Asia.

Incorrect
Students must learn to cope up with pressure.
Correct
Students must learn to cope with pressure.
Mistake 17
"Pick up a call" not "pick a call"

While "pick a call" is widely understood in parts of Africa and Asia, the standard expression is "pick up a call" or "answer a call."

Incorrect
She refused to pick my call.
Correct
She refused to pick up my call / answer my call.
Mistake 18
"According to" not "according to me"

"According to" is used to cite someone else's view. For your own opinion, use "in my opinion" or "I believe."

Incorrect
According to me, the policy is unfair.
Correct
In my opinion, the policy is unfair.
Mistake 19
Confusing "affect" and "effect"

"Affect" is usually a verb meaning to influence. "Effect" is usually a noun meaning a result. This trips up even native speakers.

Incorrect
The new policy will effect thousands of students.
Correct
The new policy will affect thousands of students.

A useful tip: Affect is the Action (verb). Effect is the End result (noun).

Mistake 20
"At the end of the day" used as filler

This expression has become a meaningless filler in many varieties of English worldwide. In good writing, every phrase must earn its place.

Incorrect
At the end of the day, education is important. At the end of the day, we need to study hard.
Correct
Ultimately, education is what matters most.

How to Improve

Five habits that will strengthen your grammar:

1. Read widely. The more standard English you absorb through books, newspapers, and academic texts, the more natural correct grammar becomes.

2. Proofread everything. Never submit any piece of writing without reading it through at least twice.

3. Learn the rules, not just the corrections. Understanding why something is wrong is more powerful than memorising the right answer.

4. Keep an error log. Write down every grammar mistake your lecturers or editors mark in your work. Review it before your next assignment.

5. Use a good reference text. A comprehensive grammar guide — such as Reader's Companion on Use of English — is an invaluable companion throughout your studies.

Grammar is not about perfection — it is about clarity. Every error you eliminate makes your writing stronger, your arguments more persuasive, and your academic work more credible.

Master these twenty mistakes, and you will write better English than the majority of graduates worldwide. That is not an exaggeration — it is a fact born of thirty years in the classroom.
