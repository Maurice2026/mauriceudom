7 Mistakes First-Time Authors Make When Publishing a Book

Hard-won lessons from decades of writing, publishing, and editing — so you can avoid the traps that derail most debut authors.

Publishing your first book is one of the most exciting and terrifying milestones in a writer's life. You have poured months — perhaps years — of work into your manuscript. The temptation to rush it into the world is overwhelming. But haste, inexperience, and misplaced priorities destroy more debut books than lack of talent ever will.

After thirty years of writing, publishing, and editing the manuscripts of other authors, I have seen the same seven mistakes repeated by first-time authors around the world — from self-published novelists in Accra and Karachi to indie authors in London and Toronto. Here they are, with practical advice on how to avoid each one.

01
Publishing Before the Book Is Ready

This is the most common and most damaging mistake. The excitement of finishing a first draft creates a powerful urge to publish immediately. But a first draft is not a finished book. It is a starting point.

Every experienced author will tell you: the real writing happens in revision. Your first draft captures the story. Your second and third drafts shape it. Professional editing polishes it. Skipping these steps means publishing a rough, unfinished product — and first impressions with readers are permanent.

The fix: After finishing your draft, set it aside for at least two weeks. Return to it with fresh eyes, revise it thoroughly, then hire a professional editor before publishing.
02
Skipping Professional Editing

Many first-time authors believe they can edit their own work. Some ask a friend or family member to proofread. Neither approach is sufficient. You cannot objectively evaluate your own writing — your brain automatically fills in gaps and corrects errors because you know what you intended to say.

A professional editor sees what is actually on the page, not what you meant to put there. They catch grammatical errors, structural weaknesses, unclear passages, and inconsistencies that you will never notice yourself.

The fix: Budget for at least a copy edit. If your budget allows, invest in a developmental edit as well. This is the single best investment you can make in your book. Professional editing services are available for authors at every level.
03
Using an Amateur Cover

You have heard the saying: never judge a book by its cover. Readers ignore this advice completely. A professional, genre-appropriate cover is essential. An amateur cover — clip art, poor typography, low-resolution images — signals to potential readers that the content inside is equally amateur.

Your cover is the first thing a reader sees, whether they are browsing Amazon, scrolling through Selar, or looking at your book on social media. It must be professional, visually appealing, and appropriate for your genre.

The fix: Hire a cover designer or use professional templates on Canva. Study the covers of bestselling books in your genre and match the visual style. Your cover should look like it belongs alongside traditionally published books.
04
Having No Marketing Plan

Many authors believe that publishing is the finish line. In reality, it is the starting gun. If you do not tell people your book exists, they will not find it — no matter how good it is. The "build it and they will come" approach does not work for books.

Marketing does not have to be expensive or complicated, but it does have to be intentional. At a minimum, you should have a plan for how you will announce your book, where you will promote it, and who your target readers are.

The fix: Start building your audience before publication. Use social media, an email list, and online communities related to your topic. Plan a launch strategy — even a simple one — that generates awareness in the first week.
05
Pricing the Book Wrong

First-time authors either price too low (hoping to attract readers with a bargain) or too high (hoping to maximise revenue per sale). Both approaches backfire. A book priced at $0.99 sends a signal that the author does not value their own work. A book priced at $25 with no author track record or reviews will struggle to sell.

The fix: Research competitive pricing in your genre and market. For ebooks, $2.99–$9.99 is the standard range. For print, $9.99–$19.99 depending on length and genre. Academic and reference books can command higher prices. Check what similar books are selling for on Amazon and match that range.
06
Ignoring Formatting

Poor formatting — inconsistent fonts, irregular spacing, missing page numbers, incorrect margins — makes your book look unprofessional and is distracting for readers. It is especially common among authors who format their books using word processors without understanding print layout requirements.

The fix: Use dedicated book formatting tools like Kindle Create (free, for Amazon), Reedsy Book Editor (free), or Atticus. If formatting feels overwhelming, hire a professional formatter. The cost is typically modest compared to the quality improvement it delivers.
07
Not Building an Author Platform

Your book is one product. Your author platform is the long-term asset. An author platform — a website, a blog, an email list, a social media presence — gives you a way to reach readers directly, build relationships, and promote future books. Authors who publish without a platform have to start from scratch with every new release.

The fix: Create a simple author website with your biography, book listings, and a way for readers to contact you or subscribe to updates. A blog with valuable content related to your book's topic drives search traffic and builds your reputation over time.
The underlying lesson: Publishing a book is not just a creative achievement — it is a professional endeavour. The authors who succeed long-term are those who treat every aspect of the process with the same care they give to the writing itself. Write the best book you can. Then edit it, design it, format it, price it, and market it with equal professionalism. Your readers — and your career — will thank you.
