Why I Wrote The Blind Gods: The Story Behind the Play

The personal inspiration, creative journey, and moral urgency behind my most powerful tragedy.

Every writer has a play that writes itself — a story so urgent that it does not wait for the right moment or the right mood. It demands to be told. The Blind Gods was that play for me. It came not from imagination but from observation — from years of watching women suffer under systems that were designed to protect everyone except them.

Where the Story Began

I did not sit down one morning and decide to write a play about injustice against women. The play grew from a slow accumulation of stories I heard, situations I witnessed, and silences I could no longer ignore. In communities across Nigeria — and across the developing world — I saw women denied inheritance, denied voice, denied justice, not because of any law of nature but because of customs that had hardened into sacred traditions over centuries.

I saw widows stripped of their husband's property by his relatives on the day of the funeral. I saw young women forced into marriages they did not choose. I saw mothers silenced in family councils where decisions about their own children were made by men who had never changed a diaper or stayed up through a feverish night. And I saw how these injustices were justified — always — by an appeal to tradition, culture, or the will of the gods.

That phrase — the will of the gods — haunted me. Because if the gods truly willed such suffering, then the gods were blind. And if the gods were not blind, then the people invoking them were lying. Either way, someone needed to say it aloud. That is what the play became.

The Writing Process

I wrote the first draft of The Blind Gods in a period of intense focus. The characters came quickly because they were not invented — they were assembled from fragments of real people I had known, taught, and listened to over decades. The protagonist carries the weight of every woman I had seen crushed by patriarchal tradition. The antagonists carry the self-righteousness of every elder who justified cruelty as custom.

But writing a tragedy is not just about anger. Anger gives you the impulse; craft gives you the play. I spent months revising the structure, refining the dialogue, ensuring that every scene built pressure toward the inevitable climax. A tragedy must feel both surprising and inevitable — the audience must not see the ending coming, but when it arrives, they must recognise that it could not have ended any other way.

The hardest part was the dialogue. I wanted the characters to speak in a way that was authentically rooted in the rhythms and idioms of the community, yet accessible to any audience. The oppressors in the play do not speak like villains — they speak like reasonable people defending reasonable customs. That is what makes them terrifying. The most dangerous injustice is always the one that sounds reasonable.

What the Play Argues

At its core, The Blind Gods makes a simple argument: no tradition that requires the suffering of innocent people deserves to survive. Culture is not a museum piece to be preserved unchanged — it is a living thing that must grow, adapt, and discard the parts of itself that cause harm. When we use tradition as a shield against justice, we are not honouring our ancestors — we are hiding behind them.

The "blind gods" of the title are not the deities of any particular religion. They are the systems — legal, cultural, social, religious — that people create and then worship as though they were divine and unchangeable. The play asks: who benefits when we refuse to question these systems? And who suffers?

The play speaks beyond any single community to every developing nation — and indeed every society — where the architecture of daily life is built on the silence of those who suffer most.

The Response

The response to The Blind Gods confirmed what I had hoped — that this was not just a Nigerian story. Readers and audiences from across Africa, from South Asia, from communities in Europe and the Americas where patriarchal traditions still shape women's lives, recognised themselves in the play. The specific customs may differ, but the pattern is universal: power justifies itself through tradition, and tradition silences those who might challenge it.

One reviewer called it "a searing indictment of patriarchal systems." Another described it as the work of a playwright who "can tickle the emotions in tragedy." These responses moved me, because they confirmed that the play had done what I intended — not merely to provoke anger, but to create empathy. To make the audience feel what it is like to be on the wrong side of a system that everyone else accepts as normal.

Why It Still Matters

I wrote The Blind Gods years ago, but its themes have not aged. In many communities around the world, women are still denied equal inheritance, still subjected to forced marriage, still silenced in decisions that affect their own lives. The legal frameworks are slowly improving, but customs change more slowly than laws. The play remains relevant because the injustice it describes has not yet ended.

Every time a young woman reads the play and says "this is my mother's story" or "this happened in my village," I know the work is not finished — both the play's work and the larger work of justice that it calls for.
