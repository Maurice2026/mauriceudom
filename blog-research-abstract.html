How to Write a Research Abstract: A Guide for Postgraduate Students

The abstract is the most important 250 words you will write. Here is how to get them right.

Your abstract is the first — and often only — part of your research that anyone reads. Journal editors use it to decide whether your paper is worth reviewing. Conference organisers use it to accept or reject proposals. Researchers scanning databases use it to decide whether to read your full article. If your abstract is weak, unclear, or poorly structured, your research may never reach its audience.

Despite its importance, the abstract is one of the most poorly taught aspects of academic writing. Many students write it as an afterthought — a hasty summary dashed off minutes before submission. This guide shows you how to write an abstract that is clear, complete, and compelling, whether you are preparing a master's thesis in Nairobi, a PhD dissertation in Delhi, a journal submission in São Paulo, or a conference paper in Berlin.

What an Abstract Must Contain

A well-structured abstract answers five questions in roughly 150–300 words (check your institution's specific requirements). These five elements form the skeleton of every effective abstract.

1. Background / Context

One to two sentences establishing the topic and why it matters. What is the broader issue or gap in knowledge that your research addresses? This gives the reader a reason to care.

2. Objective / Purpose

One sentence stating exactly what your study aims to achieve. Use clear, direct language: "This study examines..." or "This paper investigates..." Avoid vague phrases like "This paper looks at some aspects of..."

3. Method / Approach

One to two sentences describing how you conducted the research. What methodology did you use? What data did you collect? What was your sample size or analytical framework? Be specific enough for the reader to judge the credibility of your approach.

4. Results / Findings

Two to three sentences summarising your key findings. What did you discover? Include specific data points where relevant — numbers, percentages, or clear outcomes. This is the most important part of your abstract.

5. Conclusion / Implications

One to two sentences explaining what your findings mean. What is the significance? What are the practical implications or recommendations? What does your research contribute to the field?

Abstract Template

Fill-in Template
[Background] Despite growing interest in [topic], little research has examined [specific gap]. [Objective] This study investigates [specific research question/aim]. [Method] Using [methodology], data were collected from [sample/source] and analysed using [analytical approach]. [Results] The findings reveal that [key finding 1]. Additionally, [key finding 2]. [Conclusion] These results suggest that [implication], and recommend [practical recommendation or direction for future research].

Example Abstract

Sample Abstract (Social Sciences)
Background: Access to clean water remains a critical challenge in rural communities across sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia, with significant implications for public health and economic development. Objective: This study examines the relationship between community-managed water systems and waterborne disease rates in three rural districts. Method: A mixed-methods approach was employed, combining quantitative health data from district hospitals (n=2,400 patient records) with qualitative interviews of 45 community water committee members across 15 villages. Results: Communities with active water management committees reported 37% fewer waterborne disease cases compared to communities without structured oversight. Qualitative data revealed that committee training, transparent financial management, and regular maintenance schedules were the three strongest predictors of system sustainability. Conclusion: These findings suggest that investment in community governance capacity is at least as important as infrastructure investment in achieving lasting improvements in rural water access. Policy recommendations include mandatory training programmes for water committees and standardised maintenance protocols.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake: Being too vague
"This study looks at the effects of social media on students. The results were interesting and significant."
Better: Being specific
"This study examines the impact of daily social media use (>3 hours) on GPA among 800 undergraduate students across four universities. Results indicate a statistically significant negative correlation (r = -0.34, p < 0.01)."
Mistake: Including information not in the paper
Do not mention findings, methods, or claims in your abstract that do not appear in the full paper.
Mistake: Using jargon without explanation
If your abstract will be read by people outside your narrow specialty, avoid unexplained technical terms. Write for an educated but non-specialist reader.
Final checklist before submitting your abstract:

Does it answer all five questions (background, objective, method, results, conclusion)?
Is it within the required word count?
Does it contain specific results, not vague claims?
Is every sentence clear on first reading?
Does it accurately represent the full paper?
Have you included 4–6 relevant keywords?
Is it free of grammatical and spelling errors?

A strong abstract takes time to write — often longer than you expect for such a short piece. Do not treat it as an afterthought. Write it after your research is complete, revise it multiple times, and ask a colleague or editor to review it. Those 250 words will determine whether your research reaches the audience it deserves.
